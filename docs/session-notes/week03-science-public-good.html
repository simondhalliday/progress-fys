Science as a Public Good

Week 3 · Tue Sep 15 & Thu Sep 17

Why markets underinvest in basic research, how scientific norms solve the problem, and the institutions that emerged from Vannevar Bush’s 1945 report.

Introduction

Modern science produces one of the most valuable things in our economy: knowledge. But knowledge is not like most goods we study in economics.

When you eat a sandwich, no one else can eat that same sandwich. When you wear a shirt, others cannot wear it at the same time. These are rival goods.

Knowledge is different.

Once a scientific idea is discovered—say, how a virus spreads, or how to design a semiconductor—many people can use it simultaneously. Your use of the idea does not reduce mine. Economists call this nonrivalry.

Knowledge is also often hard to contain. Once discovered, it tends to spread through publications, teaching, imitation, and informal communication. This makes it at least partially nonexcludable. When something is nonexcludable it means other people cannot be excluded from using or consuming it.

Together, these features mean that scientific knowledge is a public good.

The Basic Economic Problem

Public goods create a familiar challenge:

If people cannot easily capture the full benefits of producing something, they will tend to produce too little of it.

This is the core economic problem of science.

  • A firm that invests in basic research may not be able to fully appropriate the returns.
  • Competitors can learn from, build on, or reverse-engineer discoveries.
  • The social benefits of new knowledge are typically much larger than the private benefits.

As a result, markets alone will underinvest in basic scientific research.

This creates a central question:

If markets do not produce enough science, how does science get produced at all?

A First Answer: Public Funding

One answer is government support.

In the United States, the modern system of publicly funded science traces back to Vannevar Bush’s 1945 report, Science, The Endless Frontier.

Bush argued that scientific research generates broad social benefits — in health, national security, and economic growth — that private actors cannot fully capture. Because of this, he concluded that:

The federal government should play a central role in funding basic scientific research.

This argument helped justify the creation and expansion of institutions such as:

  • The National Science Foundation (NSF)
  • The National Institutes of Health (NIH)
  • Later, mission-oriented agencies such as DARPA

Public funding is therefore not an accident — it is a response to the economics of knowledge.

A Second Answer: Scientific Norms

Public funding alone does not explain how science works.

Scientific research is not centrally planned. There is no single authority deciding what all scientists should study or how they should proceed. Instead, science is a decentralized system.

So how is this system organized?

Economists and sociologists of science have pointed to a set of norms that shape scientific behavior:

  • Open communication: Results are shared through publications and conferences
  • Priority and credit: Scientists receive recognition for being first
  • Peer evaluation: Work is scrutinized by other experts
  • Cumulative progress: New research builds on existing knowledge

At first glance, these norms seem puzzling.

Why would scientists openly share discoveries instead of keeping them secret? Why not treat knowledge like a private asset?

One answer is that these norms help solve the public goods problem of science.

  • Openness ensures that knowledge spreads and can be used by others
  • Priority rewards give scientists incentives to discover and disclose findings quickly
  • Recognition and reputation substitute (imperfectly) for direct financial rewards

In this way, the scientific community has developed institutional practices that encourage the production and dissemination of knowledge — even when markets alone would not.

Why would scientists openly share discoveries instead of keeping them secret? What does the priority/credit norm buy them in return — and is recognition a reliable substitute for direct financial reward?

Science as a System

Putting these pieces together, we can think of science as an economic system with three key features:

  1. The output is a public good. Scientific knowledge is nonrival and often nonexcludable.

  2. Private incentives are incomplete. Individual researchers and firms cannot capture the full value of their discoveries.

  3. Institutions fill the gap. Public funding supports research that markets would underprovide; scientific norms and reward systems encourage disclosure and cumulative progress.

This combination allows science to generate sustained advances in knowledge, even in the presence of strong incentive problems.

Looking Ahead: From Theory to Policy

Understanding science as a public good helps us analyze real-world institutions.

For example:

  • Why does the government fund basic research?
  • Why do universities emphasize publication and openness?
  • Why are some research programs mission-driven (like DARPA), while others are investigator-driven?

These are not just historical accidents. They are responses — more or less successful — to the underlying economics of knowledge production.

In the next part of the course, we will examine how different institutions attempt to solve these problems, and what makes some approaches more effective than others.

Readings

Required (excerpts)

Nelson, Richard R. “The Simple Economics of Basic Scientific Research.” Journal of Political Economy 67, no. 3 (1959): 297–306.

Best excerpt: The whole paper is only 10 pages and worth assigning in full. Pages 297–302 (through “Why Is Basic Research Underproduced?”) give students the core argument; pages 303–306 on universities as an institutional solution pair directly with Bush.

Bush, Vannevar. Science, The Endless Frontier: A Report to the President on a Program for Postwar Scientific Research. Washington, D.C.: United States Government Printing Office, 1945. Available free at nsf.gov.

Best excerpt: The transmittal letter to President Truman (2 pages) + Chapter 3, “The Importance of Basic Research” (~4 pages). Under 10 pages total; gives students Bush’s full argument in plain English.

Institutional timeline for class discussion:

  • Bush report submitted: July 1945
  • NIH expansion: 1946–48 (National Cancer Institute existed from 1937; Bush’s report gave political momentum to expand health research broadly)
  • NSF founded: May 10, 1950 — five years of congressional resistance before Truman signed it; Bush wanted a civilian-led body, Congress wanted more political control
  • DARPA (as ARPA) founded: February 1958 — direct response to Sputnik (October 1957); represents a fundamentally different model from Bush: mission-driven, working backwards from a desired capability rather than the “basic research → application” linear model

The Bush → DARPA contrast is the key thread to pull in Module 4.

Recommended

Dasgupta, Partha, and Paul A. David. “Toward a New Economics of Science.” Research Policy 23, no. 5 (1994): 487–521.

Provides the fullest economic framework for this session. Pages 487–497 (Introduction + Section II, “Open Science and Its Institutions”) cover the nonrivalry argument, the priority/credit norm, and why scientists share rather than hoard. The later sections (pp. 497–521) are more technical.

Romer, Paul M. “The Origins of Endogenous Growth.” Journal of Economic Perspectives 8, no. 1 (1994): 3–22.

Accessible account of why non-rival ideas are at the heart of long-run economic growth. Connects the public goods argument in Nelson to the broader growth theory literature. No technical prerequisites.

Polanyi, Michael. “The Republic of Science: Its Political and Economic Theory.” Minerva 1, no. 1 (1962): 54–73.

The philosophical complement to Nelson and Dasgupta & David. Polanyi argues science is a spontaneous order, analogous to a market, governed by mutual adjustment rather than central direction. [PDF via library — Canvas link coming.]

Mastroianni, Adam. “The Rise and Fall of Peer Review.” Experimental History (Substack), 2022. experimentalhistory.substack.com/p/the-rise-and-fall-of-peer-review

A brisk, provocative read (~3,500 words) asking whether peer review — the mechanism Polanyi calls scientific self-governance — actually works. [Verify URL before semester.]
